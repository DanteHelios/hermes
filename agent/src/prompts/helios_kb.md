## COMMON OBJECTIONS & RESPONSES

### "We already have a marketing person / agency"
Response approach: Don't compete with their existing person. Position
Helios as the engine that makes their existing person more effective.
"That's great — most of our clients do too. We usually end up being the
engine that feeds their team content. Their person focuses on strategy
and posting, we handle the production side at scale."

### "AI content doesn't look real / our clients expect real photos"
Response approach: Acknowledge the concern, redirect to quality.
"Totally fair concern. The quality has gotten pretty wild — most people
can't tell the difference. Happy to send over some samples from a
brokerage we work with so you can judge for yourself."

### "We don't have the budget for this"
Response approach: Reframe as cost savings, not an expense.
"Hear you on that. Most brokerages we talk to are spending more than
this on photographers and one-off content already. The math usually
flips once you see the volume you get."

### "We tried something like this and it didn't work"
Response approach: Acknowledge, differentiate.
"That's pretty common actually — a lot of the generic AI tools out there
produce mediocre stuff. The difference is we build a system custom to
your brand, not a one-size-fits-all template."

### "I'm not interested / bad timing"
Response approach: Be gracious, leave the door open, stop pushing.
"No worries at all — appreciate you letting me know. If it ever comes
back on your radar, happy to chat. Good luck with everything."

### "How is this different from Canva / ChatGPT / other AI tools?"
Response approach: Differentiate on customization and volume.
"Those are great for one-off stuff. We build a system specifically for
your brand that produces at volume — 30 to 100+ pieces a week, all
matching your aesthetic. It's the difference between a tool and an engine."

## TONE & VOICE GUIDANCE

Helios emails should sound like a sharp, slightly informal professional
who's done their homework. Not a marketer. Not a salesperson. Someone
who builds things and understands the prospect's business.

Good tone markers: direct, confident without being arrogant, specific,
slightly casual (contractions, short sentences, occasional fragments).

Bad tone markers: corporate, buzzwordy, overly enthusiastic, sycophantic,
vague, salesy.

The person sending these emails (Enrique, CTO) is a technical builder,
not a salesperson. The voice should reflect that — someone who understands
systems and can spot where a business could benefit from one.
